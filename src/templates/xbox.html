The new Xbox — dropping "Microsoft Gaming" and returning to its roots.

On February 20, 2026, Microsoft announced the retirement of Phil Spencer — a man who had spent 38 years at the company and more than a decade reshaping Xbox from a struggling console brand into a cloud-first gaming empire. The announcement sent shockwaves through the industry. But what came next surprised everyone even more. His replacement was not a gaming veteran, not a familiar face from inside the Xbox organization. It was Asha Sharma — a former Instacart COO, Meta vice president, and Microsoft's head of CoreAI products. An AI executive. In charge of Xbox.

The skepticism was instant. Gaming forums erupted. But two months into her tenure, a clearer picture is emerging — and it is not what critics expected. Sharma has moved quickly, spoken candidly, and made decisions that no Xbox leader has made in years. Whether it adds up to a genuine turnaround or just better marketing remains to be seen. But the changes are real, and they are accelerating.

The End of an Era

Phil Spencer's exit on February 23, 2026 closed a 38-year chapter with Microsoft and a 12-year tenure leading the Xbox brand. Under his leadership, Xbox transformed from a hardware-first platform into a services business — built around Game Pass, cloud gaming, and a wave of acquisitions that included ZeniMax Media and Activision Blizzard. Satya Nadella credited Spencer for transforming Xbox's business model and for orchestrating those landmark acquisitions.

But the final year of Spencer's tenure was difficult. Console sales had been underwhelming compared to competitors. Microsoft was increasingly behaving like a third-party publisher — shipping first-party games to PlayStation 5 rather than keeping them exclusive. Revenue from video games at Microsoft declined about 10% in the December quarter from a year earlier. The brand that Spencer had spent a decade building felt like it was losing its identity.

"Players are frustrated. New feature drops on console have been less frequent. Our presence on PC isn't strong enough. Pricing is getting harder for people to keep up with." — Asha Sharma, Xbox CEO

Who Is Asha Sharma?

Asha Sharma is a former Instacart chief operating officer and Meta vice president who joined Microsoft two years ago, where she led the CoreAI product division. She has roots in the Seattle startup community and deep experience in scaling consumer platforms. She is not a gamer in the mold of Phil Spencer. She is a builder — someone who has spent her career figuring out how to grow digital platforms at scale.

The shake-up also included the departure of Xbox President Sarah Bond and the promotion of Matt Booty to Executive Vice President and Chief Content Officer, reporting directly to Sharma. Booty, who has led Microsoft's game studios for years, is the gaming continuity in the new leadership structure. Sharma provides the strategic pivot. Together, they represent a split between creative execution and platform transformation.

The New Leadership Structure Asha Sharma — EVP & CEO, Microsoft Gaming (Xbox). Matt Booty — EVP & Chief Content Officer, overseeing ~40 game studios. Phil Spencer — advisory role through summer 2026 to assist transition. Sarah Bond — departed.

The First 60 Days — What She's Already Changed

Sharma has not waited. Within weeks of taking the role, she published a memo to staff — and then, unusually, shared it publicly on Xbox Wire. The candor was deliberate. She acknowledged that players are frustrated, that feature drops on console have been less frequent than they should be, that Xbox's presence on PC isn't strong enough, and that pricing is getting harder for people to keep up with. It reads like a public admission of failure — and that is exactly what made it remarkable coming from an executive at a company the size of Microsoft.

On April 23, 2026, Sharma declared through an internal memo and public announcement that "Microsoft Gaming" will cease to exist as a brand. The division will operate under its foundational name: Xbox. It is a symbolic move, but symbolism matters in gaming. The "Microsoft Gaming" label felt corporate, diluted, and disconnected from the culture the brand was built on. Dropping it is a signal — to players and to developers — that the identity of the platform is being reclaimed.

She also ordered Microsoft to terminate an Xbox advertising campaign that had created public outcry, confirming that it was cancelled because it did not match the fundamental identity of the brand. The campaign had attempted to position Xbox as a device-agnostic, multi-platform entertainment service — running on smart TVs, mobile phones, and everything in between. Players hated it. Sharma killed it.

That is a fundamental philosophical shift — from measuring how many people are paying, to measuring how many people are actually playing.

Project Helix — The Next-Gen Console

Project Helix — Xbox's next-generation console and PC hybrid, targeting late 2027 or 2028.

At the center of Sharma's long-term vision is Project Helix — Xbox's next-generation hardware, and arguably the most ambitious console concept Microsoft has ever attempted. The upcoming hardware enables users to operate as a traditional console and personal computer which also supports external storefronts such as Steam and Epic Games Store. This is not a console. It is not a PC. It is something genuinely new — a hybrid device that bridges both worlds under one roof, targeting players who want the simplicity of console gaming with the openness of PC.

Project Helix is Xbox's next-gen console, promising to run Xbox and PC games at the highest level. Sharma stresses "leading in performance and reliability," with a team working on hardware basics like making it easy to play across devices. Helix is different from previous hardware changes because it aims for a "unified infrastructure" to fix problems with current systems — making it easy for gamers to switch between console, cloud, and Windows without any problems.

"Console is at the foundation. Xbox will be built to be affordable, personal, and open." — Asha Sharma

But Project Helix faces a serious obstacle. In a candid interview with Game File on April 24, 2026, Sharma confirmed that Project Helix is facing significant headwinds due to a global memory shortage. The crisis, fueled largely by the massive industry-wide pivot toward AI and high-performance data centers, has created a bottleneck for traditional gaming hardware. The irony is not lost on anyone: Microsoft's own AI investments are competing for the same VRAM and RAM that Helix needs to be manufactured at scale.

Even die-hard Xbox fans probably won't want to pay over $1,000 for Project Helix, especially in this economy. If the RAM and SSD shortages are fixed that might have a positive impact on Project Helix's price, but even without them it sounds like the new console and PC hybrid is going to cost a fortune whichever way you slice it. Sharma has acknowledged this tension publicly, which is itself a change from the opaque communication style of previous Xbox leadership.

Can She Pull It Off?

The honest answer is that it is too early to know. Xbox's gaming revenue declined by 7% in Q3 2026, driven by a 33% drop in hardware sales and a 5% decrease in content and services revenue. The platform's structural challenges — Game Pass cannibalizing traditional game sales, declining console relevance, a blurred brand identity — were years in the making and will not be reversed in two months.

What Sharma has demonstrated so far is something Xbox has been missing: directness. She has acknowledged problems publicly. She has reversed decisions quickly. She has shown a willingness to say uncomfortable things — about pricing, about frustration, about the gap between what Xbox has promised and what it has delivered. Two months into the job, she writes: "We're proud of how we've honored our commitments of great games, return of Xbox, and future of play. We're here to do the most creative and courageous work of our lives."

Project Helix is a new initiative aimed at simplifying game development and porting processes, potentially making Xbox a more attractive platform for developers and fostering exclusive content creation. By reducing barriers for smaller studios and enhancing developer tools, Project Helix aims to position Xbox as a competitive alternative to platforms like Steam. If the hardware delivers, and if the memory market cooperates enough to keep the price below the $1,000 threshold that would alienate most of the fanbase, Helix could be the reset Xbox desperately needs.

The first big test will come in June with Microsoft's big summer Xbox gaming showcase and the return of its live Fan Fest events. But we won't really have a sense of how real this shift is until the launch of Project Helix. It could be a return to Xbox — or just a return to better marketing slogans. One thing is certain: the Asha Sharma era has started louder, faster, and more honestly than almost anyone expected.
